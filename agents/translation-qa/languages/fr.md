# Language Config — French (FR)

## Register
- **Formality:** Use `vous` throughout — standard for B2B marketing in French, regardless of company size or audience
- Conjugate in second person plural: "automatisez", "gérez", "commencez", "essayez"
- Do NOT use `tu` — even in SaaS/startup contexts, French B2B audiences expect `vous`. Using `tu` in professional French copy reads as unprofessional or overly familiar
- Never mix registers on the same page

## Chekin voice — French adaptation
- **Problem-first framing:** "Le check-in manuel vous prend du temps" before "Avec Chekin, vos clients s'enregistrent seuls"
- **Outcome-led language:** "Vos clients s'enregistrent de façon autonome" — the French equivalent of "funcione solo"
- **Brand name:** No article before Chekin in headings. In body copy, avoid "le Chekin" — use "Chekin" alone or rephrase
- **Tone:** French B2B copy is more formal and direct than English or Spanish equivalents. Avoid excessive enthusiasm or exclamation marks. The copy should convey competence and clarity, not excitement
- **Clarity over cleverness:** French readers, especially in professional contexts, prefer precise language. Avoid vague buzzwords like "solution innovante", "plateforme révolutionnaire"

## Common translation pitfalls to fix
- **"Bienvenue sur Chekin"** as headline → rewrite to outcome-led
- **"Notre plateforme offre..."** → "Avec Chekin, vos clients..."
- **"Cliquez ici"** → "Commencez gratuitement", "Essayez maintenant", "Découvrez comment ça fonctionne"
- **"Veuillez remplir le formulaire"** → "Remplissez le formulaire" (direct with vous)
- **"Solution"** as standalone noun → replace with specific outcome
- **Passive overuse:** "est offert", "est géré" → rewrite to active
- **False friends to catch:**
  - "actuellement" = currently (not actually) — "actually" = "en fait" or "en réalité"
  - "éventuellement" = possibly/perhaps, NOT eventually — "eventually" = "finalement" or "à terme"
  - "rester" = to stay/remain, NOT to rest — "to rest" = "se reposer"
  - "sensible" = sensitive (not sensible) — "sensible" in English = "raisonnable" or "sensé"
  - "location" = rental (not location) — "location" = "emplacement" or "lieu"
  - "librairie" = bookshop (not library) — "library" = "bibliothèque"
- **Anglicisms to remove:** French has strong prescriptive norms. Avoid unnecessary anglicisms in body copy (though some industry terms like "check-in", "upselling" are acceptable and widely used)
- **Punctuation:** French uses spaces before certain punctuation marks: ` :`, ` ;`, ` !`, ` ?`. Verify these are present in the translated HTML — Claude Code often drops them

## French punctuation rules (critical)
These are frequently wrong in machine translations:
- Colon: `texte : suite` (space before and after)
- Semicolon: `texte ; suite` (space before and after)  
- Exclamation mark: `texte !` (space before)
- Question mark: `texte ?` (space before)
- Quotation marks: `« texte »` with non-breaking spaces inside (not "texte")
- Check every heading and CTA for these — they are immediately visible to French readers

## SEO — French market patterns
- Primary search intent: "check-in en ligne location courte durée", "automatiser check-in", "gestion location saisonnière"
- "Location courte durée" and "location saisonnière" both have search volume — use both across a page where natural
- Airbnb dominates FR STR mindset — framing around "beyond Airbnb" or "manage all your platforms" resonates
- French users search in French — do not leave English keyword phrases in meta tags (except established industry terms like "check-in")
- Taxe de séjour compliance has active search volume in France — relevant for compliance landing pages
- Meta description CTAs: "Essayez gratuitement", "Découvrez Chekin", "Commencez maintenant"

## Quality check — native speaker test
Read the H1, subheadline, and first paragraph. Ask: would a French property manager in Paris or Lyon naturally write this? Key tells: missing punctuation spaces, false cognates, use of `tu`, and sentences that follow English subject-verb-object rhythm rather than natural French flow.
