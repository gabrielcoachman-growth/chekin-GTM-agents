# Language Config — German (DE)

## Register
- **Formality:** Use `du` (informal) for property manager and STR host audiences — Chekin's target segment in Germany skews toward modern, tech-forward operators who expect the startup/SaaS `du` register
- Conjugate accordingly: "automatisierst", "verwaltest", "fängst an", "probierst"
- Exception: if the page targets traditional hotel chains or corporate hospitality groups, switch to `Sie` — indicated in filename or folder (`/hotel-enterprise/`). With `Sie`: "automatisieren Sie", "verwalten Sie"
- Never mix `du` and `Sie` on the same page — this is a serious error to German readers

## Chekin voice — German adaptation
- **Problem-first framing:** "Manueller Check-in kostet dich Zeit und Nerven" before "Mit Chekin erledigen deine Gäste den Check-in selbst"
- **Outcome-led language:** "Deine Gäste checken selbst ein" — the German equivalent of "funcione solo"
- **Brand name:** No article before Chekin in headings. In body copy, "das Chekin" or "die Chekin-Plattform" are grammatically needed but rephrase to avoid them in headlines
- **Tone:** German B2B copy values precision, reliability, and concrete specifics over emotional appeals. Replace "amazing" and "revolutionary" with specific numbers or outcomes where possible. "15 Minuten bis zur ersten Buchung" beats "schnell und einfach"
- **Compound nouns:** German creates compound nouns that can become unwieldy. Break them up where they exceed 3 components or where they obscure meaning: "Kurzzeitvermietungsverwaltungssoftware" → "Software für Kurzzeitvermietung"

## Common translation pitfalls to fix
- **"Willkommen bei Chekin"** as headline → rewrite to outcome-led
- **"Unsere Plattform bietet..."** → "Mit Chekin erledigen deine Gäste..."
- **"Klicken Sie hier"** (or "Klick hier") → "Jetzt starten", "Kostenlos ausprobieren", "So funktioniert's"
- **"Bitte füllen Sie das Formular aus"** → "Formular ausfüllen" or "Füll das Formular aus" (du register)
- **"Lösung"** as standalone noun → replace with specific outcome
- **Passive overuse:** "wird angeboten", "wird verwaltet" → rewrite to active. German tolerates passive voice more than English or Spanish, but marketing copy should be active
- **False friends:**
  - "aktuell" = current/present, NOT actual — "actual" = "tatsächlich" or "eigentlich"
  - "eventuell" = possibly/perhaps, NOT eventually — "eventually" = "schließlich" or "letztendlich"
  - "sensibel" = sensitive, NOT sensible — "sensible" = "vernünftig" or "sinnvoll"
  - "Kontrolle" = control/monitoring, not necessarily "control" in the UI sense — "dashboard" is better kept in English
- **Hyphenation of compound nouns with brand names:** "Chekin-Integration", "Chekin-Dashboard", "Chekin-Plattform" — always hyphenate when combining with German nouns
- **Capitalization:** ALL German nouns are capitalized. Check that machine translation hasn't lowercased nouns: "die automatisierung" → "die Automatisierung"

## German regulatory context (for compliance pages)
- **Meldepflicht** — mandatory guest registration in Germany. Use exact term.
- **DSGVO** (Datenschutz-Grundverordnung) — German term for GDPR. Use DSGVO, not DSGVO/GDPR.
- **Touristensteuer / Kurtaxe** — varies by city/region. Flag for local review if the page references specific German tax compliance.

## SEO — German market patterns
- Primary search intent: "Online-Check-in Ferienwohnung", "Check-in automatisieren", "Ferienvermietung Software"
- "Ferienwohnung" (holiday apartment) and "Ferienvermietung" have strong search volume in Germany
- German users search almost exclusively in German — do not leave English keyword phrases in meta tags
- Compound keywords are common in German search: "Online-Check-in-Software", "Ferienwohnungsverwaltung" — include natural compounds in meta description
- Trust signals matter more in DE than other markets — if the page references DSGVO compliance or data security, ensure it appears in meta description
- Meta description CTAs: "Jetzt kostenlos testen", "Mehr erfahren", "Jetzt starten"

## Quality check — native speaker test
Read the H1, subheadline, and first paragraph. Ask: would a German property manager in Berlin or Munich naturally write this? Key tells: incorrect noun capitalization, mixing `du`/`Sie`, non-hyphenated brand compounds, false cognates, and sentences following English syntax rather than German verb placement rules (verb-second in main clauses, verb-final in subordinate clauses).
