# Language Config — Czech (CS)

## Register
- **Formality:** Use `vy` (formal, written as `Vy` when addressing the reader directly) for B2B property manager audience — standard in Czech professional and SaaS marketing
- Conjugate in second person plural (formal): "automatizujete", "spravujete", "začněte", "vyzkoušejte"
- Do NOT use `ty` (informal) — Czech B2B marketing defaults to formal address. Using `ty` reads as overly casual and unprofessional in this context
- Never mix formal and informal on the same page

## Chekin voice — Czech adaptation
- **Problem-first framing:** "Ruční check-in vám zbytečně bere čas" before "S Chekin si vaši hosté odbavení vyřídí sami"
- **Outcome-led language:** "Vaši hosté se odbavují sami" — the Czech equivalent of "funcione solo"
- **Brand name:** In Czech, brand names of foreign origin are typically used without declension in headlines. In body copy, Czech grammar requires declension — "Chekin" stays undeclined as it ends in a consonant cluster that resists Czech declension patterns. Use "s Chekin" (not "s Chekinem") — flag if the translation has added Czech case endings to the brand name
- **Tone:** Czech B2B copy values directness and practicality. Avoid flowery language or emotional marketing tropes. Lead with function and concrete benefit

## Czech-specific grammar issues to check
Czech is a highly inflected language. Machine translations frequently produce errors in:

1. **Case endings:** Czech has 7 grammatical cases. Check that nouns, adjectives, and pronouns agree in case with their context. Common errors:
   - Nominative used where accusative or genitive is needed
   - Brand names given incorrect Czech case endings (see above)

2. **Aspect pairs:** Czech verbs have perfective/imperfective aspect pairs. Marketing copy should prefer perfective for completed outcomes ("automatizujte" — complete action) vs imperfective for ongoing states. Check CTAs especially.

3. **Word order:** Czech has relatively free word order but topic-comment structure is important. Translated sentences often follow English SVO rigidly — rewrite if they sound unnatural.

4. **Diacritics:** Czech uses háček (ě, š, č, ř, ž, ý, á, í, ú, ů, etc.). Verify that diacritics have not been stripped from the translation — this is a common machine translation output error and immediately signals poor quality to Czech readers.

5. **"Pronájem" vs "nájem":** Both mean rental/lease but "nájem" is the legal term and "pronájem" is more common in everyday and marketing use. Use "pronájem" in marketing copy.

## Common translation pitfalls to fix
- **"Vítejte v Chekin"** as headline → rewrite to outcome-led
- **"Naše platforma nabízí..."** → "S Chekin si vaši hosté..."
- **"Klikněte zde"** → "Začněte zdarma", "Vyzkoušejte nyní", "Zjistěte, jak to funguje"
- **"Prosím vyplňte formulář"** → "Vyplňte formulář"
- **"Řešení"** as standalone noun → replace with specific outcome
- **English false cognates in Czech tech translations:**
  - "aktuální" = current (correct Czech word), but often used where "skutečný" (actual/real) is meant
  - "eventuálně" = possibly — NOT eventually. "Eventually" = "nakonec" or "postupně"

## SEO — Czech market patterns
- Czech STR market is smaller but growing, centered on Prague and tourism regions (Český Krumlov, spa towns)
- Primary search intent: "online check-in ubytování", "automatizace check-in", "správa krátkodobého pronájmu"
- Czech users search in Czech — do not leave English meta content except for established terms (check-in)
- Prague-specific compliance (Airbnb/STR regulations, registration obligations) may be relevant for compliance pages
- Meta description CTAs: "Vyzkoušejte zdarma", "Zjistěte více", "Začněte hned"

## Quality check — native speaker test
Read the H1, subheadline, and first paragraph. Ask: would a Czech property manager in Prague naturally write this? Key tells: missing or incorrect diacritics, wrong case endings, rigid English sentence structure, and brand name incorrectly declined.
